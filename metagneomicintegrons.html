The first integron was discovered in 1989 by Ruth Hall and H W Stokes.

  • What are integrons

    • Integrons are genetic elements capable of capturing and expressing open reading frames (ORFs) embedded in gene cassettes (Cambray, Guerout et al. 2010, Gillings 2014). The term “integron” or “DNA Integration elements” was first proposed by Stokes and Hall (1989) to describe a novel type of mobile genetic elements carrying different ARGs. They were predicted to be mobile element due to their wide distribution in different locations such as plasmids and transposons. The integrons are familiar for their major role in acquisition, expression and dissemination of ARGs. Although integrons are categorised as mobile genetic elements, they are not mobile in their own right . They rely on other genes like transposases or recombinases which are usually found in the cassette arrays or site adjacent to the integrase genes (Gillings 2014)

  • Structure of integrons

    • Integrons are composed of three basic features which are essential for its gene capturing and expression functionality: a gene encoding a DNA integrase (intI); an integron associated recombination site, attI; and a gene cassette promoter, Pc, which expresses the inserted gene cassettes (Figure 1).

  • Types of integrons

    • Integrons are broadly classified into two groups: mobile integrons (MIs) or resistance integrons (RIs) and chromosomal integrons (CIs), also called superintegrons (SIs) (Fluit and Schmitz 2004). MIs are associated with mobile DNA elements (transposons or plasmids) and usually possess a few cassettes in the cassette array. The MIs typically carry the ARGs which confer resistance to most classes of antibiotics, whereas the CIs bear contain GCs containing genes encoding proteins predicted to carry out a range of diverse functions
